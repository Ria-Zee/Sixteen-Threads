## Ile-Olu — The Neutral City (Starting Point)

A city built where four ancient kingdoms' borders meet, governed by no kingdom and respected by all seven. Its streets are built wide enough for two ox-carts side by side, because its founders understood that trade requires the ability to pass each other without touching. The architecture is a deliberate layering of all seven kingdoms' styles — Yoruba compound homes share walls with Akan stool houses, Swahili coral-stone facades face Zulu beehive structures, Kikuyu thatch meets Mandinka mud-brick in the market quarter. The effect should be chaos. Instead it is a visual argument that coexistence is an aesthetic choice before it is a political one.

The High Council meets in a circular building with no head of table — seven seats, equidistant, each facing the center. The session lamp at the center burns a specific blend of seven ritual oils. When the lamp goes out, the session ends. No exceptions.

**Current state:** Tense. The diplomatic quarter is full. Every kingdom has recalled or upgraded their Ile-Olu representation. The market is 30% quieter than normal — traders are holding stock rather than selling, which is what markets do when they sense disruption. The High Council has not officially acknowledged the Aso-Ijo's damage publicly, which means everyone knows through other channels, which means the deliberate silence reads as either incompetence or strategy and nobody can tell which.

## The Road to Ori-Oke — Five Days Through Wrong Landscape

The party travels northeast from Ile-Olu through terrain that is becoming increasingly unreliable. The wrongness is not dramatic — it does not announce itself. It accumulates.

**Day One:** A baobab tree flowering three months out of season. Its blossoms fall upward. Not float — fall upward, as if the tree's gravity is inverted. The blossoms are perfectly formed. They smell correct. They land in the branches of a shorter tree above and rest there.

**Day Two:** A village where the children all dreamed the same dream last night. They will not say what it was. The adults in the village, when asked, say the children have been quiet since dawn and they are not sure if they are afraid or waiting. The village elder gives the party dried fish and says: "When the cloth is wrong, the children hear it first. Their ears are closer to where it was made."

**Day Three:** A river crossing. The water is cold on the left bank, warm on the right. The temperature line is as sharp as a cut — step one foot into cold, step the other foot into warm. The riverbed is the same riverbed. Something about the river has stopped agreeing with itself.

**Day Three Night:** Seku seen in the dark, speaking to someone with no shadow. "A prayer," he says in the morning.

**Day Four:** A forest where the trees are the right trees but in the wrong arrangement. Not dramatically wrong — the teak is where the iroko should be, the fig where the teak was. As if the forest memorized its own layout incorrectly. Animals are moving through it as if nothing is unusual, which suggests they can't tell the difference, which suggests the arrangement was never as fixed as humans assumed.

**Day Five:** The mountain. The Mandara plateau. Three paths visible from below. The party arrives by the Northern Path, which they did not choose. The path chose them — or more precisely, the ground arranged itself so that the Northern Path was the one that was there when they stepped forward. The shrine is visible from below. It appears intact.

## The Ori-Oke Shrine — Detailed Map

### The Outer Court (Patio of Noise)
Stone flags, worn smooth by centuries of pilgrims. Fire pits for cooking and warming — cold, but recently swept. The sweeping pattern is systematic, not casual: whoever swept this knew the traditional pattern for ritual preparation. Seven stone seats for kingdom representatives, placed in the same equidistant arrangement as the High Council chamber in Ile-Olu — this is not coincidence, the Council copied Ori-Oke. The seats have been dusted. Not cleaned. Dusted — quickly, recently.

### The Middle House (House of Voices)
Built from iroko and red sandstone, the walls thick enough that sound behaves differently inside — voices carry in some directions and absorb in others depending on the speaker's position. This is deliberate: the keeper's chair is placed at the acoustic center, where any voice directed at the keeper arrives at full clarity while any voice not directed at the keeper murmurs into the walls. Privacy by architecture.

The consultation chair of carved iroko faces the entrance. In it: Mama Moremi. Eyes open. Focused on the middle distance. Breathing regularly. She is wearing her keeper's cloth — white cotton with indigo borders, the keeper's specific weave pattern identifying her role. The cloth is unwrinkled. She has been in this chair long enough to become still, not long enough to become disheveled. Between seven and twenty days.

The seven bronze mirrors face inward, reflecting each other infinitely. In theory they show an infinity of the room. Currently, six of them do. The seventh — the one on the northern wall — shows a different room. Not a reflection. A room in Orun. The room is not empty.

The 200 clay tablets: names of everyone who made a covenant at Ori-Oke. The tablets are in chronological order. The most recent entries are 310 years old — the last renewal ceremony. But there is one tablet that is out of sequence: a tablet wedged between entries from 60 years ago, inscribed with a single name in a handwriting style that does not match any other tablet. The name has been partially obscured — scraped away — but two letters remain: **O** and a character that could be **b** or **d**.

### The Inner Sanctum (Belly of the Cloth)
The door is unlocked. The ritual washing basin is prepared — water infused with osun (camwood), efun (white chalk), and a third herb that Thandiwe identifies as *Acacia senegal*, used in Zulu healing tradition for spiritual protection before encounters with the dead. This is unusual: Acacia senegal is East African, not typically used in Yoruba shrine practice. Someone with different herbal knowledge prepared this basin, or Mama Moremi has been reading widely.

The Aso-Ijo hangs between its two iron hooks. Nine meters of silk-cotton, the threads so fine they are difficult to count with the naked eye. The tear is visible — a diagonal wound near the upper left, clean-edged, not frayed. This is not a rip. It is a cut. The missing section's absence is more alarming than the tear: the threads around that area are not snapped but *carefully unraveled* back six centimeters in each direction, as if someone unstitched the section the way a tailor would remove a patch from a garment. The work is expert. It took time. It was done in this room.

[SEALED]: The missing section's outline, when examined carefully, is shaped like a map. The seven-kingdom alliance's geographic territories, compressed into a palm-sized piece of cloth. Each kingdom's contribution to the original covenant is woven into that section. It is not just a piece of cloth — it is the architectural record of the agreement itself. Without it, the Aso-Ijo can be patched but the covenant cannot be structurally restored. Ìmọlẹ̀ Tuntun took the agreement, not just the fabric.

### The Fire Keep (Ina Ayo — Fire of Joy)
A cylindrical tower of granite, wider at the base, narrowing toward the open top where the eternal flame burned. The inner walls are black with three centuries of soot in a halo pattern — the flame burned true and clean, never smokier than the moment of the covenant renewal.

The ash in the fire basin is cold — not recently extinguished cold but weeks-cold, compressed slightly by its own weight. Someone has not walked through this ash. They avoided it deliberately, stepping around the basin's edge to place the nail exactly in the center. The nail stands precisely vertical. It is the most careful thing in this room. Whoever placed it was making a statement they wanted read correctly.

The nail is Nri-style: the specific twist pattern on the upper third, the composition of the iron alloy (slightly higher carbon content than Oyo or Mandinka forge work), the finish — Nri smiths leave a specific mark in the last hammer strike. This nail is approximately forty years old based on oxidation patterns at the base. Thandiwe dates it accurately. Akin dates it accurately and then says nothing about what that dating means for the current king of Nri's culpability. Zawadi notices he says nothing.

## Orun — The Spirit Realm (Accessible After Act Two)

Not a place that can be described fully in advance because it takes the shape of the traveler's reference points. What the party will encounter:

**The Road of Recall:** The path between Aye and Orun that an uninvited traveler must walk. It looks like every road the traveler has ever walked, composite and shifting. The difficulty is not physical — it is mnemonic. The road asks the traveler to remember things they would prefer to forget. Resisting the remembering makes the road longer.

**The Holding Court:** Where displaced spirits wait — not imprisoned, but suspended. Mama Moremi is here, in a replica of her consultation chair, awake and entirely aware, unable to return because the thread connecting her to her body has been held by someone who had the authority to take it.

**The authority to take a spirit's thread** belongs to three entities: the spirit's own choice, a god's direct action, and a living person who holds a specific covenant-object belonging to the spirit. The covenant-object in question is a small carved wooden figure that Mama Moremi made thirty years ago and gave to someone she trusted. That person used it to pull her thread. This is not a metaphysical abstraction — it is a deliberate, specific act of betrayal by someone Moremi knew personally.
