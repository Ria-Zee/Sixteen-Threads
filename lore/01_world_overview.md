## The Two Realms

The universe of Orun Chronicles is built on a single architectural truth: **reality has two faces, and they were never meant to see each other directly.**

**AYE** is the mortal world. Red laterite earth and black cotton soil. The smell of groundnut soup and harmattan dust. Drumbeats that travel faster than horses. Markets so loud they have their own weather. Aye is the realm of sweat, consequence, and time — things happen here, age, and stop. It is beautiful in the specific way of things that are temporary.

**ORUN** is the spirit realm. Not heaven, not hell — those are too simple. Orun is more like memory given geography: a place where everything that has ever happened continues to exist in some form, where the dead pick up conversations they dropped in Aye, where gods conduct politics without the embarrassment of being watched by mortals. Everything in Orun is exactly what it is, without the mercy of pretense.

Between them: **the Aso-Ijo** — the Covenant Cloth.

## The Aso-Ijo: The Covenant Cloth

Woven at the first moment of creation by **Obatala** — Yoruba god of white cloth, purity, and the human form — the Aso-Ijo is not a metaphor. It is a physical object: nine meters of woven silk-cotton fabric, each thread a compressed lifetime, the entire textile smelling faintly of rain on dry earth regardless of weather. It hangs in the innermost chamber of the Shrine of Ori-Oke, suspended between two iron hooks that have never rusted in three thousand years.

The cloth does not keep Orun and Aye apart by force. It keeps them apart by *agreement* — every thread is a surrendered thing: a memory, a name, a heartbeat, a voice. Each thread is a promise. The cloth is, literally, a covenant made textile.

**When the cloth is intact:**
- The dead stay in Orun and remember fondly
- Spirits require invitation to cross into Aye
- Gods speak through intermediaries — priests, diviners, dreams
- Time flows in one direction
- The laws of each realm hold within their borders

**When the cloth tears, those guarantees dissolve — proportionally to the size of the damage.**

Current damage: a 40-centimeter diagonal rip near the upper-left quadrant, plus a missing palm-sized section — not torn, *removed*.

[SEALED]: The missing section was removed by someone with authorized inner-chamber access. Only four people in history have ever held such access: Mama Moremi (current keeper), Baba Seun (previous keeper, deceased twenty years), Elder Chibuike Okafor of Nri (age 78, currently in the Nri palace), and one other whose name was deliberately excised from every record approximately sixty years ago. The excision itself is a clue.

## The Ori-Oke Shrine — Structure

**The Outer Court (Patio of Noise):** Open air, swept daily. Currently abandoned — but swept within the last week by an unknown hand.

**The Middle House (House of Voices):** Where the keeper receives visitors. Contains: a consultation chair of carved iroko wood, a speaking drum audible only after sunset, seven bronze mirrors facing inward, and 200 clay tablets inscribed with every person who has ever made a covenant here. Currently: intact. Mama Moremi found here, in the consultation chair, in the state of kũhĩa njĩa — spirit-displaced, non-responsive, breathing.

**The Inner Sanctum (Belly of the Cloth):** Where the Aso-Ijo hangs. Access requires keeper's permission and ritual washing. Currently: door unlocked, ritual water basin already prepared — either by Moremi before her displacement or by someone after. The cloth is present, torn.

**The Fire Keep (Ina Ayo — Fire of Joy):** Stone tower housing the eternal flame, burning for three centuries on covenant-energy alone. Currently: extinguished. In the cold ash: one iron nail, Nri-style forge work, approximately forty years old, standing upright.

[SEALED]: Moremi prepared the ritual water herself before her spirit was taken. She knew someone was coming. The open door is an invitation, not a breach. She wants someone to enter, read the cloth's remaining threads, and find what she found before her spirit was pulled away.

## Cosmological Rules

1. **Spirits cannot lie in Orun** — but they can refuse, speak in riddles, or answer a different question than the one asked.
2. **Divination reads probability, not certainty** — player choices override divination outcomes.
3. **The Aso-Ijo cannot be rewoven without the missing section** — a patch creates a permanently weakened point requiring renewal every seven years.
4. **Spirit-displacement (kũhĩa njĩa) can be reversed** — requires one person to voluntarily enter Orun uninvited, find the displaced spirit, and guide it back. The person who enters must give Orun a reason to let them leave.
5. **The Nri iron nail is a claim marker, not a weapon** — in Igbo tradition, iron driven into sacred fire means "this place is under our jurisdiction." It is an aggressive political act. Akin reading it as a declaration of war is technically incorrect. Strategically, he is not entirely wrong.

[SEALED — THE THIRD PARTY]: The faction responsible for the cloth's damage calls itself **Ìmọlẹ̀ Tuntun** — "New Light" in Yoruba. They believe the Aso-Ijo has prevented necessary evolution by keeping realms separate. Their goal: merge Aye and Orun permanently, believing it will end death and produce a civilization of gods and mortals as one. They are not wrong that this would happen. They are catastrophically wrong about whether it would be good. The "shadowless figures" appearing near the party are Ìmọlẹ̀ Tuntun couriers — they straddle the torn boundary, half in each realm, which is now possible precisely because of the damage they caused.
