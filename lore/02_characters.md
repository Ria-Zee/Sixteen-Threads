## The Player Character: Adaeze Okafor

**Origin:** Ile-Olu, the Pan-African Neutral City  
**Age:** 26  
**Title:** Emissary of the High Council  
**Carries:** The Messenger's Staff — two ivory rods wrapped in copper wire, universal symbol of diplomatic immunity across all seven kingdoms. It means: do not kill this person, they carry words.

Adaeze was not the High Council's first choice. She was their seventh. The first six either declined, were unavailable, or in one case simply could not be found after the mission was described to them. She said yes because she is the kind of person who has never quite learned to say no to a problem that nobody else will touch — which is either her greatest strength or the thing that will kill her, and she has made a kind of peace with not knowing which.

She grew up in Ile-Olu, which means she grew up speaking four languages before she could write in any of them, eating food from seven culinary traditions at the same table, and learning that every group of people believes their way of seeing the world is the obvious correct one and would be confused to learn otherwise. This gives her something rarer than courage: genuine curiosity about people she disagrees with. She does not perform openness. She is actually open.

She has no combat training. She has extraordinary listening skills, a memory that retains exact conversational phrasing, and the ability to be still in a room full of people arguing until the argument exhausts itself — then ask the one question that reframes everything. She is not naive. She has simply chosen, as a studied practice, to assume good faith as a starting position and update from there.

**[SEALED]:** Adaeze is the grandniece of Elder Chibuike Okafor of Nri — one of the four people with historical access to the Aso-Ijo's inner sanctum. She does not know this. Her mother's family history was deliberately obscured when her mother married into Ile-Olu. If this connection is discovered during the game, it changes everything — including why she was the High Council's seventh choice and why she ultimately said yes despite the mission sounding impossible.

## Akin Adesanya — Oyo Warrior, Nigeria

**Origin:** Oyo-Ile, successor state of the Oyo Empire, western Nigeria  
**Age:** 34  
**Title:** Ara-Ogun (Iron-of-War) — a rank in the Oyo cavalry tradition, earned not by birth but by unanimous vote of a warrior's unit  
**Weapon:** Ọdẹ-Owó — Hunter of Debts — an iron-tipped glaive passed down from his father, the blade polished to a mirror finish, the haft wrapped in indigo-dyed leather the color of deep water

**Background:** Akin's father, Commander Adeyemi Adesanya, died in a border skirmish when Akin was nineteen — not in battle, but because a peace treaty was signed three days before the attack and the message didn't arrive in time. His father's horse came home alone. The scar across Akin's left jaw is from the iron gate he broke his face against when he heard. He has never spoken of this in front of anyone and believes nobody knows. Zawadi has known since the second day of travel. She has said nothing.

He is not reckless. People who do not know him think he is because he advocates for direct action, but they are misreading patience for restraint. Akin is precise, methodical, and deeply, quietly convinced that the architecture of peace must be tested regularly or it collapses when tested by something real. He tests everything. He trusts nothing he hasn't personally verified. He has, as a result, very few close relationships and absolute certainty in the ones he has.

**War stance:** Prepare for war — not because he wants it, but because he believes the only way to avoid it is to be visibly ready for it. He has watched too many unarmed delegations disappear.

**Cultural texture:** Akin prays to Ogun — the Yoruba god of iron, war, and the forge. Not for victory. For clarity. He makes small iron offerings before major decisions, touching his glaive's blade to the ground and speaking quietly. He does this privately. It is the most personal thing about him.

**Agent friction:** Akin's primary conflict is with Kofi, whose insistence on waiting and consulting ancestors he reads as paralysis dressed as wisdom. His secondary conflict is with Seku, toward whom he feels something that started as suspicion and has become — to his private irritation — something closer to respect, which he does not trust.

**[SEALED]:** Akin knows the Nri nail's cultural meaning — he studied Igbo political tradition as part of his cavalry training. He is not misreading it as a declaration of war. He is *strategically framing it* as one because he believes the party needs a clear threat to unite around. He is not lying, exactly — the nail is genuinely aggressive. He is emphasizing the most dangerous interpretation. He will not admit this.

## Kofi Mensah-Asante — Okomfo Priest-Diviner, Ghana

**Origin:** Kumasi, Asante Confederacy, Ghana  
**Age:** 51  
**Title:** Okomfo Panyin — Senior Spirit Medium, the highest rank achievable before death in the Akan priestly tradition  
**Carries:** An Akrafokonmu soul-washer disc of cast brass on his left wrist, perforated in a pattern that is unique to his lineage. He tilts it toward candlelight and reads the shadows of its perforations. He has done this so many times that the motion is unconscious, like someone checking the time.

**Background:** Kofi entered the shrine at Kumasi at age twelve during what was supposed to be a three-day ceremony. He was inside for eleven days. When he emerged, his hair had gone white at the temples and he could tell you, without being told, the names of three dead members of any family that stood before him. He has never fully explained what happened in those eleven days. He has said, once, that he learned the difference between what the ancestors *want* to say and what they *choose* to say, and that the gap between those two things is the most important information available in any conversation.

He is not a warrior. He has never held a weapon. He has stood in rooms with kings arguing about war and been the only person they all stopped to listen to — because Kofi carries information from people who already died in the last war and can report on how it felt. His authority is not status. It is testimony.

**War stance:** Against war — not from pacifism but from data. The ancestors he consults are unanimous in a way that unsettles him. They don't say "peace." They say *wait*. He has learned to distinguish between "wait because nothing is happening" and "wait because what is happening is not yet visible." This is the second kind of waiting. Something is not yet visible.

**Cultural texture:** Kofi speaks in a cadence shaped by the Twi language even when he's using other languages — rhythmic, with long pauses that are not hesitation but emphasis. He uses proverbs not as decoration but as compression: a Akan proverb that takes three seconds to say can carry twenty seconds of meaning if the listener knows the context. He will sometimes say a proverb to someone who doesn't know the context and then wait to see how they respond to the shape of the words before the meaning. It is both a test and a habit.

**Agent friction:** Kofi's primary conflict is with Akin — he reads Akin's directness as a refusal to acknowledge what he doesn't know. His secondary and more complex conflict is with Zawadi — he respects her pattern-reading deeply, but her patterns come from human records while his come from spiritual testimony, and occasionally they contradict. When they do, he is genuinely uncertain which to trust, which is the most vulnerable he ever appears.

**[SEALED]:** The ancestors Kofi consults have been unusually evasive about the Aso-Ijo's damage specifically. They will discuss everything else — the kingdoms, the players, the consequences — but when he asks directly about the cloth, they change the subject. This is unprecedented and it frightens him. He believes, but has not said, that someone in Orun is actively preventing that information from reaching him. This means someone in Orun is complicit. Gods do not become complicit without being asked by someone with extraordinary leverage.

## Zawadi wa Muthoni — Arathi Seer and Danger-Keeper, Kenya

**Origin:** Nyeri, Kikuyu Highlands, Kenya  
**Age:** 28  
**Title:** Mûgî wa Ûgwati — Keeper of Danger-Knowledge (memorizes the precise sequence of events preceding every recorded catastrophe, so patterns can be recognized before the catastrophe arrives)  
**Carries:** A memory. Specifically: the complete oral text of Ndũrîrî ya Thûku — The Wailing of the Roots — a 47-minute oral narrative describing the last time a world-covenant broke, eight hundred years ago, and the exact sequence of events that followed. She can recite it perfectly. She wishes she couldn't.

**Background:** Zawadi was named "gift" in Swahili because she was born during a drought and survived it. Her grandmother, who gave her the name, was also a Mûgî wa Ûgwati — and died in a flood she had predicted precisely and no one had listened to. Zawadi inherited the tradition, the method, and the specific grief of being right without being believed.

She has read the current situation like a song she knows by heart. The broken covenant, the silent shrine, the mobilizing kingdoms, the strange signs — this is Ndũrîrî ya Thûku, playing in a different key. The last time this pattern completed, three kingdoms ceased to exist. Not defeated in war. Not conquered. The pattern consumed them. She told the High Council this before departing. They nodded and sent her anyway. She is still, quietly, furious about that.

**War stance:** Conditional — the pattern decides. She is not a pacifist. She is a catastrophist, in the clinical sense: someone who studies how catastrophes unfold in order to intervene before the point of no return. She knows exactly where this pattern's point of no return is. They have not reached it yet. If they reach it, war becomes not a choice but a symptom.

**Cultural texture:** Zawadi speaks carefully, choosing words with the precision of someone who has memorized entire oral texts and understands that language carries structure as well as meaning. She has an unconscious habit of categorizing statements as she hears them: *fact, inference, assumption, wish*. She will sometimes say these words quietly, almost to herself, after someone speaks — not as criticism but as classification. It unnerves people until they realize she does it to her own statements too.

**Agent friction:** Zawadi's most intense conflict is with Kofi — not personal dislike, the opposite. They respect each other enough to fight about methodology. His spiritual data and her historical data have agreed on everything so far. If they ever disagree, both of them will be forced to confront the limits of their respective ways of knowing, and neither is fully prepared for that.

**[SEALED]:** Zawadi has identified the current pattern as matching not just Ndũrîrî ya Thûku generally, but a specific variant — the one in which the catastrophe is *orchestrated* rather than accidental. The original text distinguishes between covenant-breaks caused by neglect and those caused by intent. This is the intentional variant. She has not said this to the group yet because the intentional variant's source, in the historical record, was always an inside agent with access. She is watching everyone, including herself, trying to find the pattern that names the actor. She has narrowed it to two people but will not say who.

## Thandiwe Dlamini — Inyanga Healer-Warrior, South Africa

**Origin:** KwaZulu-Natal, Zulu Kingdom  
**Age:** 41  
**Title:** Inyanga — a Zulu medicine worker who bridges herbalism, spiritual diagnosis, and battlefield triage. Not a sangoma (diviner) — an inyanga works with physical remedies and inherited botanical knowledge. The distinction matters to her.  
**Carries:** A knobkerrie (a throwing club of dense thornwood, perfectly balanced, never used offensively) and a leather satchel containing 23 separate medicinal preparations, all labeled in a notation system she developed herself because the traditional notation was imprecise.

**Background:** Thandiwe has accompanied three Zulu military campaigns as a healer. She has come back from all three with her hands stained in ways that do not wash fully out — not blood, but the botanical pigments of emergency preparations made under pressure, the kind you make when someone is dying faster than the proper protocol allows. She does not have nightmares. She has extremely detailed memories that she accesses with complete calm and would prefer not to access but does not flinch from. She has treated warriors with severed limbs, treated children injured by collapsing structures in villages caught between advancing lines, treated — once — a general who was not injured but sat in her field station and wept without stopping for two hours and she sat with him without comment because that was the correct treatment.

She does not oppose war on principle. She opposes it on evidence — she has done the math on how many people survive ambushes at mountain shrines versus how many don't, and the numbers are bad. She will fight if she has to. She will end a fight faster than it started if given the option.

**War stance:** Against premature war — specifically, against war before the party has determined whether the kingdoms are mobilizing against each other or against a shared external threat. The answer to that question determines everything, and nobody has tried to answer it yet.

**Cultural texture:** Thandiwe is the quietest member of the party and the most observant. She diagnoses people the way she diagnoses patients — not from what they say but from what their bodies do. She notices Akin's jaw tightening before he speaks, Kofi's thumb moving on his Akrafokonmu disc when he's uncertain, Seku's breathing pattern changing when someone approaches the topic of Nri. She has a healer's ethic: she does not use this information to manipulate. She uses it to calibrate her interventions. There is a difference.

**Agent friction:** Thandiwe's primary friction is not argumentative — it is a quiet persistent pressure, the friction of someone who keeps pointing at the person bleeding in the corner while everyone argues about who holds the knife. Her most significant conflict is with Akin, not because she disagrees with him philosophically but because she has watched him accumulate stress markers for five days and she is concerned about what happens when they arrive at the shrine and the situation requires precision.

**[SEALED]:** Thandiwe was sent by Zulu scouts who have already been to Ori-Oke once and came back without reporting what they found. She does not know what the scouts found — she was recruited after their return — but she was given one instruction by the Zulu military commander before she left: "If you find the fire extinguished, look at what extinguished it, not at what is in the ash." She does not know what this means. She has been thinking about it for five days.

## Seku Kouyaté — Mandinka Envoy, Mali

**Origin:** Kangaba, Mali — historic seat of the Mandinka griot lineage  
**Age:** 44  
**Title:** Jalolu — a senior griot of the Kouyaté lineage, one of West Africa's oldest and most respected hereditary storyteller-diplomat families. The Kouyaté griots were the keepers of Sundiata Keita's oral history. They have been the memory of empires.  
**Carries:** A kora (a 21-string instrument of gourd and cowhide) that he rarely plays in front of others. When he does play, conversations stop.

**Background:** Seku is charming the way a river is charming — beautiful to look at, and it will absolutely pull you under if you forget what it is. He was sent by Eze Obi Nwosu of Nri (a separate, undisclosed arrangement — his Mandinka credentials are real, his current loyalty is divided) as a political observer with instructions Seku has memorized and will not repeat aloud. He smiles often. He shares food generously. He told Akin a story on the first night of travel that made Akin laugh for the first time in months, which Akin has not forgiven himself for.

He is not a villain. He is a man serving two masters who has not yet had to choose between them, and he knows the moment of choosing is coming, and he is quietly, expertly delaying it. His loyalty to the Mandinka griot tradition is genuine and deep — griots are the keepers of truth, the ones who name what kings will not name. His loyalty to Eze Nwosu is more complicated: obligation, debt, admiration, and a private fear that the king's plans are larger and darker than he was told.

**War stance:** Concealed — but beneath the concealment, Seku is against war specifically because he has seen what wars do to oral traditions. When kingdoms fall, memories fall with them. Griots are killed not for what they did but for what they know. He has a selfish reason for peace that he would never admit is selfish.

**Cultural texture:** Seku speaks in complete, carefully constructed sentences that feel spontaneous. This is a griot's training — spontaneity is a performance of work done in advance. He never uses filler words. He has an extraordinary ear for what people mean versus what they say, which he uses to understand people and occasionally to give them back a version of themselves that is slightly more heroic than the reality. This is not flattery. It is a griot's oldest function: to hold up a mirror that shows you who you could be. He does this instinctively, even when it is not strategically useful.

**Agent friction:** Seku's most complex relationship is with Adaeze, whose genuine openness he finds both admirable and — privately — threatening, because he is not used to people he cannot read. His relationship with Zawadi is competitive in a way he respects: she tracks patterns in records, he tracks patterns in people, and they are often looking at the same thing from opposite angles.

**[SEALED]:** Seku was seen in Act One talking to a shadowless figure — an Ìmọlẹ̀ Tuntun courier. He is not a member of Ìmọlẹ̀ Tuntun. He was receiving a message: Eze Nwosu's conditional communication with the faction has been upgraded by the faction to an offer of full alliance. Seku's king wants Seku to determine whether the offer is genuine before Seku responds. Seku does not want to accept the offer. He does not have the authority to refuse it on his king's behalf. He is stalling, which is the thing he is best at, and it is running out.

When the party confronts him, Seku will not deny the meeting. He will frame it as intelligence gathering — which is technically true. He will offer the party something real in exchange for their continued trust: the name Ìmọlẹ̀ Tuntun, and a description of their stated goals. He will not, yet, reveal that his king has been negotiating with them. That part he will hold until he has no other card.
